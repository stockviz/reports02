Period: September to December 2025

Executive Summary

Ravindra Energy Limited (REL) is transitioning from a renewable energy developer into a dual-engine growth company—pursuing aggressive expansion in Renewable Energy (RE) and Electric Vehicles (EVs) via its subsidiary Energy/Motion (EIM). While near-term financials face challenges, the structural moves in operations, strategy, and governance signal long-term value creation. However, risks around execution, scalability of EV business, capital intensity, and dependence on policy support remain material.

Tailwinds (Growth Drivers & Strategic Strengths)

1. Strong Growth in Renewable Energy Business

  • Significant capacity ramp-up:
    • Operating capacity grew from ~158 MWp (Sept ’25 guidance) to 187.3 MW (Nov 2025 update).
    • Commissioned 135 MW DC (100 MW AC) under Maharashtra’s MSKVY Phase 1 — a major milestone.
    • Pipeline continues with 45 MW under construction and 182 MW AC under development, including 120 MW LOA under MSKVY Phase 3.
  • Diverse revenue streams:
    • EPC projects (11 MW Akhadabalapur, 6 MW third-party EPC underway).
    • Revenue from solar trading increased to 13.86 MU (H1 FY26) — up from 6.38 MU (H1 FY25).
  • Regulatory tailwind from KUSUM Scheme, state DISCOMs’ rural feeder solarization, and PPA agreements with MSEDCL/HESCOM, reducing off-take risk.

2. EV Business Gaining Traction

  • First commercial sales achieved:
    • 91 e-tractors sold as of November 2025 (up from 125 in presentation, indicating potential rephasing).
    • Order book expanded to 185 units, with LOI from India’s largest cement company for 45 e-tractors under long-term freight contract.
  • Infrastructure rollout:
    • Launched two battery swapping stations (JNPA port & NH-44 NCR) — validating BaaS model.
    • Partnerships with ICICI Bank for project financing and FOTON (China’s top CV OEM).
  • EV Assembly Plant in Pune:
    • MoU signed with Maharashtra Govt; ₹100 crore first phase investment, targeting vehicle roll-out by June 2026.

3. Profitability and Balance Sheet Improvement

  • Consolidated Standalone PAT (Q2 FY26): ₹31.72 Cr vs. ₹2.14 Cr YoY; H1 PAT: ₹56.16 Cr — significant improvement driven by large EPC deals.
  • Networth rise:
    • Standalone networth: ₹407.5 Cr (as of Sept’25) vs ₹350.3 Cr in March ’25.
    • Strong equity funding (PE investment via EIM or subsidiary structuring).
  • Reduced receivables: Trade receivables dropped to ₹16.98 Cr (FY25), indicating better collections.

4. Strategic Governance & Leadership

  • Appointment of independent directors (e.g., Mr. Apurva Chandra) and audit committee reconstitution signal strong corporate governance.
  • Presence of industry veterans (Narendra & Vidya Murkumbi, ex-Renuka Sugars), and auto sector expertise (ex-Ashok Leyland, Tata).
  • Aggregation of RE project SPVs (e.g., REL MSKVY Solar Project 9–15), improving project financing and tracking.
Headwinds & Execution Risks

1. EV Business: Still in Nascent, High-Burn Stage

  • Negative profitability:
    • EIM reported ₹1.7 Cr PAT loss in H1 FY26, despite ₹44.7 Cr revenue and ₹3.9 Cr gross margin.
    • Implies aggressive Selling & Distribution (S&D) and Infrastructure Capex leading to negative margins.
  • Slow sales ramp:
    • Only 91 units sold by Nov ’25 vs. 125 in Sep guidance — suggests delivery or market acceptance challenges.
  • Capex intensity:
    • Each battery swapping station costs ₹35–40 lakhs, and ~10 stations needed; total cost may exceed ₹4 Cr, requiring sustained financing.

2. Heavy Reliance on Policy & Subsidies

  • MSKVY and KUSUM schemes are state-sponsored, making project economics vulnerable to regulatory change or delay.
  • Tariff decline: Net average tariffs fell from 7.16 ₹/unit (2017) to 2.94–3.18 ₹/unit for future projects — compression due to lower bidding.
  • Any removal of capital subsidies or tax benefits for rural solar or EVs could impair ROI.

3. Capital Structure and Leverage Risk

  • Sharp increase in borrowings:
    • Total consolidated borrowings rose from ₹189.9 Cr (FY25) to ₹388.67 Cr (Sept 2025) — a ~105% increase in 6 months.
    • Debt funded RE expansion and EV investments in a high-interest-rate environment.
  • While networth also grew, debt/equity ratio nearly doubled, increasing financial risk.

Conclusion & Investment Thesis

Bull Case: Dual Growth Engines Converge

  • REL can become a leading rural solar player with 500+ MW capacity and top-tier heavy EV logistics provider.
  • EV business, once scaled, could command much higher valuation (EV + BaaS model).
  • Strong leadership, clear strategy, and MoUs with state governments and large customers de-risk path-to-scale.

Bear Case: Execution & Cash Burn

  • EV division may consume excessive capital, delaying profitability.
  • Policy changes or grid curtailment/revenue realization issues in RE.
  • Competitive bidding may further erode tariffs, affecting long-term margins.
