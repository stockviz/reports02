Executive Summary

HEC Infra Projects Limited is demonstrating a strong recovery trajectory with robust financial and operational performance in Q2 FY26. After a period of restructuring post-2020, the company has returned to sustainable, profitable growth, driven by disciplined project selection, margin focus, and diversification across core infrastructure sectors.

Despite challenges in working capital management and government receivables, the company is taking proactive steps to mitigate risks. With increasing policy tailwinds in transmission & distribution (T&D), renewable integration, battery energy storage systems (BESS), and rural water infrastructure, HEC Infra is well-positioned for multi-year growth. However, scalability constraints remain due to limited bank credit lines and execution bandwidth.

1. Tailwinds – Positive Catalysts

A. Supportive Policy & Sectoral Tailwinds

  • Jal Jeevan Mission (JJM): Strong government push for rural water distribution is translating into new municipal contracts (e.g., recent ₹4.32 crore order from AMC).
  • Green Hydrogen & BESS Push: The government is poised to mandate Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) for solar/wind projects. HEC’s first BESS project worth ₹7.15 crore signals emerging opportunity capture.
  • RDSS, PM-KUSUM Schemes: These programs are driving higher capex in power distribution, smart grid upgrades, and EPC work — all within HEC’s core competency.
  • Focus on MSME Payments: Improved government payment timelines post-2020 due to MSME protection policies aid cash flow predictability.

B. Growth in Order Book & Diversification

  • Q2 FY26 New Orders: ₹62.53 crores, including:
    • ₹28.75 cr – 66 kV substation & underground cabling (BGP Infra)
    • ₹7.15 cr – BESS implementation (Advait Energy)
    • ₹26+ cr – AMC water infrastructure projects
  • Diversified Portfolio:
    • T&D (66–220 kV systems)
    • Water distribution & augmentation
    • Battery Energy Storage Systems (emerging)
  • Reduced Government Dependency: Management actively pursuing 50:50 public-private split in revenue mix.

C. Strong Financial Growth with Profitability Discipline

  • Q2 FY26 Revenue: ₹40.82 Cr (+96.8% YoY)
  • Net Profit: ₹2.33 Cr (+82.1% YoY)
  • EBITDA Margin: 9.45% (stable despite growth)
  • Management committed to maintaining margins even if it means forgoing high-volume, low-margin projects (e.g., declined ₹100–200 Cr bids in prior quarters).

D. Improving Operational Efficiency & Processes

  • Centralized quality control from HO
  • Upgraded accounting systems and change in auditors for transparency
  • Focus on refining SOPs, procurement, and project execution post-COVID restructuring
2. Headwinds – Challenges & Constraints

A. Working Capital & Cash Flow Pressure

  • Operating Cash Flow: Negative ₹77.56 Cr in H1 FY26 vs. ₹377.54 Cr in H1 FY25
  • High Current Assets (₹9,459.77 Cr): Driven by:
    • Trade receivables (₹3,177 Cr)
    • Loans and advances (₹30 Cr up from ₹7 Cr) — largely vendor prepayments
  • Delayed Government Payments: Retention amounts delayed by up to 1.5 years (e.g., from GETCO).
    • However, not due to performance issues — purely bureaucratic delays.

B. Limited Financing Capacity

  • No borrowings currently (zero on balance sheet), but reliance on non-fund-based facilities.
  • Non-fund-based limits pending enhancement.
  • Management expects increased limits “next quarter” to support aggressive growth.
  • Current interest rate: ~9.1%, expected to reduce to 8.5% or lower with new credit rating.

C. No Long-Term Strategic Projects Due to Tenure & Cash Lock-Ups

  • Avoids projects above 2-year duration due to:
    • Long procurement lead times (e.g., 3.5 years for 400 kV transformers)
    • High bank guarantee burden
  • Focused only on short to medium-term EPC contracts (9 months to 2 years) for faster cash conversion.

5. Strategic Outlook & Differentiation

Core Strengths:

  • End-to-End EPC Capability: Design, procurement, installation, testing, commissioning.
  • Diversified Verticals: One of few EPC firms working in power, water, and BESS — reduces cyclical exposure.
  • Selective Bidding Discipline: Refusing large but unprofitable projects; ensuring healthy IRR and margins.

Strategic Initiatives:

  1. Backward Integration: Evaluating acquisitions in transformers and electrical components to improve margins.
  2. De-Risking from Government PSU Delays: Shifting focus to departments with faster payment cycles.
  3. Talent & Capex Build-Up: Investing in manpower and systems to scale operations sustainably.
6. Investor Communication & Governance
  • Regular earnings calls (since March 2025), indicating improved transparency.
  • Independent review of financials confirms no material misstatements.
  • Management actively engaging with investors, addressing concerns on cash flows, margins, and growth.
Conclusion: Buy with Caution – High Growth, Manageable Risks

Rating: Accumulate (with monitoring)

  • Upside Drivers:
    • Policy momentum in BESS, green hydrogen, water infrastructure
    • Consistent order inflows and execution
    • Margin discipline enabling sustainable profitability
  • Downside Risks:
    • Liquidity constraints until credit limits are enhanced
    • Government receivable delays affecting cash flow
    • Scalability bottleneck due to talent and capital

Investment Thesis:

HEC Infra is a re-rating story emerging from a period of operational and financial restructuring. It is now executing a high-margin, sustainable growth model rather than chasing size. With strong visibility into order inflows and favorable policy tailwinds, the company has 3–5 years of above-sector-average growth potential. Investors should monitor Q3 bank limit enhancements, operating cash flow improvement, and geographic diversification as key inflection points.
