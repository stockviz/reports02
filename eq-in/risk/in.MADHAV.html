Investment Analysis of Madhav Marbles and Granites Limited (MADHAV)

Ticker: MADHAV (NSE): 515093 | ISIN: INE925C01016 | CIN: L14101RJ1989PLC004903
Industry: Natural Stones & Building Materials | Location: Udaipur, Rajasthan
Reporting Period: Quarter ended September 30, 2025 (Unaudited)

✅ Executive Summary

Madhav Marbles and Granites Limited reported mixed financial performance in Q2 FY26 (July–September 2025), with strong standalone profitability but a deteriorating consolidated picture, reflecting operational challenges at subsidiaries. While core operations (Granite & Stone Division) remain stable and asset-light, profitability is being offset by heavy interest costs, negative EBITDA in consolidated results, and poor cash flows from operations. The company continues to rely on subsidiaries for incremental revenues, but their financial performance is weak, increasing group-level risks.

Despite solid governance and transparent disclosures (e.g., e-voting, auditor review), growth prospects appear muted unless structural issues—especially in subsidiary performance and working capital—are addressed.

📈 Tailwinds – Positive Drivers

1. Profitable Standalone Operations

  • Q2 FY26 Net Profit (Standalone): ₹3.11 crores (vs. ₹4.03 cr YoY), despite declining revenue.
  • Earnings per Share (EPS): ₹0.03/qtr (₹0.18/yearly) – positive and stable, with no diluted-impact dilution.
  • The Granite & Stone Division, while small, is the only profitable segment and contributes all standalone earnings.

2. Low Capital Intensity in Core Business

  • Standalone capital employed in Granite division: ₹1,009 cr (avg. over last year)
  • Generates revenue of ~₹685 crores/year, indicating efficient use of capital.
  • No major equity issuance: paid-up capital unchanged at ₹89.47 crores since at least March 2025.

3. Power Generation Unit Adds Value

  • Power division contributed ₹51.71 crores in segment revenue (Q2 FY26).
  • Generated pre-tax and pre-interest profit of ₹38.84 crores, one of the few bright spots.
  • Utilizes internal generation likely for captive use, reducing electricity costs.

4. Strong Corporate Governance

  • Timely disclosures to BSE/NSE, including AGM voting results and unaudited results within same day.
  • Remote e-voting conducted with third-party scrutiny (Ronak Jhuthawat & Co.), ensuring investor participation.
  • All resolutions passed with >98% voting in favor, indicating shareholder alignment.
  • Annual General Meeting (Sept 30, 2025) approved audited financials and related-party transactions, enhancing credibility.

5. High Institutional Participation in Voting

  • Though promoters did not attend AGM, public shareholders holding ~23% of shares voted remotely, showing investor engagement despite low float.
📉 Headwinds – Key Challenges

1. Consolidated Losses & Deteriorating Subsidiary Performance

  • Consolidated Q2 Net Loss: ₹71.21 crores (vs. ₹33.91 cr YoY loss) → worsening trend.
  • Consolidated EPS: –₹0.49 per share, signaling major operational drag from subsidiaries.
  • Subsidiary losses are material:
    • Two subsidiaries report combined net losses of ₹20.42 lakhs and ₹106.51 lakhs for quarter/year-to-date.
    • These subsidiaries have revenue of only ~₹10.44 lakhs, indicating cost-heavy, unproductive entities.
  • Auditors explicitly note that subsidiary results “have not been reviewed” and are based on management certification only.

2. Negative Operating Cash Flows

  • Standalone CFO: –₹1.60 crores (Q2) and –₹1.71 crores (H1 FY26) — alarming burn rate.
  • Driven by large increases in receivables (+₹3.64 cr) and inventories build-up (+₹3.11 cr).
  • Consolidated CFO: –₹57.12 lakhs (Q2) despite non-core income — shows core operations cannot fund themselves.

3. High Debt and Interest Burden

  • Interest expense: ₹5.41 crores (Q2 standalone), which consumes entire EBIT (~₹24 cr).
  • Debt levels relatively low on standalone books, but consolidated finance cost nearly doubled to ₹9.44 crores, suggesting off-balance sheet or inter-company borrowing.
  • Leverage risk evident as consolidated net loss (-₹43.75 cr) largely stems from interest on debt.

4. Poor Working Capital Management

  • Trade receivables surged to ~₹1,045 crores (standalone), likely from slow collections or aggressive credit sales.
  • Inventory levels increased to ₹2,939 crores, now 3x higher than revenue in the quarter → suggests obsolescence or overstocking risk.
  • Inventory turnover ratio declined sharply, potentially tying up capital in unsold stone slabs.

5. Lack of Growth Momentum

  • Revenue from operations (Standalone Q2 FY26): ₹684.35 cr, down from ₹792 cr in Q1 FY26 (June 2025).
  • Declining trend suggests soft demand, especially in domestic real estate, which drives stone purchasing.
  • No expansion, dividend, or buyback activity observed — reinvestment stalled.
